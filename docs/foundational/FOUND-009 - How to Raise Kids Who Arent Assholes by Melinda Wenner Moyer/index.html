How to Raise Kids Who Aren't Assholes: Science-Based Strategies for Better Parenting

Evidence-based approaches to raising kind, resilient, honest children through emotional literacy and induction

By Melinda Wenner Moyer

Why It Matters

Parents can intentionally shape children's character through evidence-based strategies emphasizing emotional literacy, perspective-taking, and intrinsic motivation. Raising kind, resilient, honest children requires deliberate effort grounded in developmental psychology rather than intuition.

Analysis & Insights

From Colorblind to Anti-Racist Parenting

Moving beyond silence about race to active conversations about racism and privilege

üí°

Key Insight

"Ignoring race prevents racism is contradicted by research. When parents remain silent, children learn race is taboo and shame-based. Anti-racist parenting requires explicit, ongoing conversations about race, racism, and privilege starting in early childhood. The goal is raising culturally competent, anti-racist individuals who navigate a diverse world with empathy."

Intrinsic Motivation vs. External Rewards

Understanding that external rewards undermine motivation for inherently satisfying activities

üí°

Key Insight

"External rewards undermine intrinsic motivation for tasks that could be inherently satisfying. Fostering intrinsic motivation requires three key elements: autonomy (meaningful choices), mastery (appropriately challenging tasks), and purpose (connecting activities to larger values). This approach produces more durable engagement and genuine enthusiasm."

Induction: Connecting Actions to Impact

Explaining how actions affect others to build genuine empathy and moral development

üí°

Key Insight

"Traditional discipline focuses on punishment without helping children understand why their behavior matters. Induction‚Äîexplaining how actions affect others‚Äîis foundational for moral development. When parents help children see that hitting hurts both physically and emotionally, children develop genuine empathy rather than fear-based compliance."

The Authoritative Parenting Balance

Combining high warmth with high expectations for optimal child development

üí°

Key Insight

"Authoritative parenting combines high warmth with high expectations, differing from authoritarian (high expectations, low warmth) or permissive (high warmth, low expectations) styles. This balance produces better academic performance, fewer risky behaviors, stronger relationships, and better emotional regulation."

Explicit Values Transmission

Directly teaching children what matters through repeated conversations and discussions

üí°

Key Insight

"Children do not absorb values through osmosis; they require repeated, direct conversations about what matters. Whether discussing kindness, anti-racism, gender equality, or honesty, parents must explicitly name and explain values through media discussions, open-ended questions, and revisiting topics regularly."

Actionable Framework

Building Emotional Literacy

Develop children's capacity to recognize, name, and regulate emotions‚Äîfoundational for empathy

1
LABEL

emotions constantly throughout daily routines ('You seem excited about the party'; 'I'm frustrated because I'm running late')

2
AVOID

dismissing feelings (don't say 'Don't be sad' or 'Calm down') even when emotions seem disproportionate

3
VALIDATE

without condoning ('I see you're angry, AND hitting isn't okay')

4
ASK

open-ended questions about emotions ('How did that make you feel?'; 'Why do you think she was upset?')

5
HELP

problem-solve ('What could you do next time you feel this way?')

6
MODEL

emotional expression ('I'm frustrated about work, so I'm going to take a walk')

7
REVISIT

emotions in stories/media ('How do you think that character felt?')

Applying Induction-Based Discipline

Help children understand how their actions affect others, building intrinsic prosocial motivation

1
PAUSE

and breathe before responding (prevents reactive punishment)

2
ACKNOWLEDGE

the child's feelings ('I see you're frustrated')

3
DESCRIBE

the behavior factually ('You hit your sister') without judgment

4
CONNECT

to impact on others ('When you hit, it hurts her AND makes her sad')

5
ASK

the child to consider the other person's perspective ('How do you think she felt?')

6
PROBLEM-SOLVE

together ('What could you do differently next time?')

7
FOLLOW

up later if the child is too upset to process in the moment

Fostering Growth Mindset

Shape children's beliefs about ability and effort to increase resilience and motivation

1
PRAISE

effort, not ability ('You worked hard on that'; NOT 'You're so smart')

2
USE

'yet' ('You can't do it *yet*'; 'You haven't mastered that *yet*')

3
TIE

success to effort ('Your practice paid off'; 'You figured it out by trying different strategies')

4
AVOID

praising for easy tasks (signals that challenge = failure)

5
REFRAME

failure as learning ('What did you learn from that?'; 'That's useful information for next time')

6
MODEL

growth mindset ('I'm not good at this *yet*, but I'm going to practice')

7
CELEBRATE

effort over outcomes ('I'm proud of how hard you tried')

Implementing Authoritative Parenting

Balance warmth and boundaries to create secure, well-adjusted children

1
ESTABLISH

clear, non-negotiable rules (safety, respect, honesty)

2
EXPLAIN

the reasoning ('We don't hit because it hurts people')

3
LISTEN

to the child's perspective ('Tell me what happened')

4
ACKNOWLEDGE

their feelings even while maintaining the boundary

5
APPLY

natural/logical consequences (related to the misbehavior, not punitive)

6
AVOID

psychological control (shame, withdrawal of love, guilt-tripping)

7
RECONNECT

after consequences ('I love you; I didn't like the behavior')

8
REVISIT

rules periodically as children develop

Having Explicit Values Conversations

Intentionally transmit values about kindness, anti-racism, anti-sexism, and integrity

1
START

early (even young children notice race, gender, power dynamics)

2
USE

media as a springboard (pause during TV/movies to discuss stereotypes)

3
ASK

open-ended questions ('Why do you think that character did that?'; 'What do you notice about who has power in this story?')

4
SHARE

your own values ('In our family, we believe...'; 'I think it's unfair when...')

5
AVOID

lecturing (kids tune out; dialogue is more effective)

6
REVISIT

topics repeatedly (values are internalized through repetition, not one conversation)

7
MODEL

the values (children do as you do, not as you say)

8
ADMIT

when you don't know ('That's a great question; let's figure it out together')
